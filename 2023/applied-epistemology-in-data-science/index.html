Applied Epistemology in Data Science

Jun 7, 2023  
  • epistemology
  • data-science
  • probability
  • forecasting

Epistemology is the branch of philosophy concerned with the nature, sources, limits, and methods of knowledge. It explores fundamental questions such as what constitutes justified and true beliefs.

Applied epistemology is the practical application of these theories about knowledge to specific domains or real-world situations. In this post, we will examine mathematical modelling and data science through the lens of applied epistemology.

Mathematical modeling objectives

We can identify three broad goals of mathematical modeling across many fields:

  • Understanding likely future events or outcomes (Prediction or Forecasting), e.g. credit risk scoring or SKU level demand forecasts.
  • Understanding the likely effects of interventions (Causal Discovery), e.g. understanding the impact of a subscription fee increase on lapse rates.
  • Determining what is the “best” way to achieve an objective (Optimal Control), e.g. ordering the timeline to maximise engagement.

One area where mathematical modeling is relatively uncommon is understanding the contributing causes of a unique past event - this mode of inquiry is more often found in history writing, criminal investigations and legal practice.

Prediction is in some important respect the easiest as it’s a constituent part of other two objectives. It can also often use the ambient data about the process.

Causal Discovery generally requires being able to predict the outcome of interest both in the regime with the intervention and in the regime without the intervention. The causal effect is then ceteris paribus the difference between the two. Causal discovery is very significantly constrained by data availability. Heterogeneous treatment effects are both rare and hard to detect.

Optimal Control – finding the “best” intervention according to some criteria requires good estimates of the causal impact of each candidate intervention.

Probabilistic statements

It’s almost universal to use probabilistic statements. For forecasting questions the answers might look something like this:

  • My party is at least 90% likely to win the next election.
  • The probability you will survive to age 80 is 60%.
  • The coin is 50% likely to land heads.
  • There’s a 35% chance of rain tomorrow.

But do these probabilities always mean the same thing? The table below shows the diverse colloquial meanings these statements can have:

Statement Colloquial Meaning
My party is at least 90% likely to win the next election. I’m very confident my party will win
The probability you will survive to age 80 is 60%. Out of 10,000 insured men forty-one years old living in a given country and not engaged in certain dangerous occupations we expect 6,000 to still be alive in 40 years.
The coin is 50% likely to land heads. If we were to flip this particular coin over and over, in the very long run it would come up heads exactly half the time
There’s a 35% chance of rain tomorrow Out of 100 days when the forecaster tells us there will be 35% chance of rain, it rains on approximately 35.

Interpreting probability

Frequentist interpretation: the probability of an event \(A\) in a reference class \(B\) is the relative frequency of realised occurrences of \(A\) within \(B\), i.e. \(\frac{|A|}{|B|}\) as \(|B| \rightarrow \infty\).

Subjective interpretation: your degree of belief in \(A\) is \(p\) if and only if \(p\) units of utility is the price at which you would buy or sell a bet that pays \(1\) unit of utility if \(A\) occurs and 0 if it does not. It is generally required that these bets should be coherent or not admit arbitrage Note that it follows immediately that \(p(A\,|\,B) = \frac{p(A\cap B)}{p(B)}\), where \(p(A\,|\,B)\) represents a bet that pays \(1\) if \(A\) and \(B\) occur and is called off if \(B\) doesn’t occur. , i.e. \(p(A \cup B) = p(A) + p(B)\) if \(A \cap B =\emptyset\).

Subjective probabilities are historically associated with Bayesian updating on evidence, but there is a good argument Bayesian updating is logically independent from coherence cf. F. Ramsey’s conditionalisation (1931) vs. so called “diachronic” conditionalisation, e.g. Teller 1973 .

We are already both frequentist and subjectivist

Our everyday thinking about probabilities reflects both approaches, as shown in this expanded table:

The probability you will survive to age 80 is 60% Out of 10,000 insured men forty-one years old living in a given country and not engaged in certain dangerous occupations we expect 6,000 to still be alive in 40 years. Frequentist (mostly)

Handling singular events

Frequentist probabilities make sense for situations when a large reference population exists or it is possible to generate sequences by repeating an experiment with the same initial conditions. They can not be reasonably used for “singular” events.

Singular events would include specific elections, sports matches or even next day’s weather at a particular location. This would naturally include any global events.

Subjective probabilities deal with individuals’ beliefs and can be easily stated for singular events. The downside is that the only requirement for these is to be coherent, i.e. admitting no arbitrage in the price structure of the hypothetical bets. They don’t need to be “true”.

A modern view on subjective probabilities introduces the concept of calibration. One’s subjective probabilities \(p\) are calibrated if for all the events where \(p(A) = x\), \(Pr(A\,|\, p(A) = x) = x\) or the frequentist definition \(\frac{|A|}{|B|}\) where the reference class of events \(B\) corresponds to the events where the subjective probability \(p(A)=x\).

A weather forecast service would be calibrated at 0.3 if it rains on approximately 30 out of 100 days when the forecast chance of rain is 30%.

The notion of calibration is not sufficient â€” a forecaster predicting the long run probability of rain each day (the so called the climatological frequency), regardless of current meteorological conditions, would still be calibrated but only marginally helpful.

We want the forecaster to vary their forecasts depending on conditions as much as possible (or to be sharp) while maintaining calibration.

Working with expert forecasts

Once we have identified an expert or a model that produces good quality forecasts, we can set our subjective probabilities equal to theirs!

It would make sense to limit this only to the domain where expert’s performance has been objectively verified - a concept of “repeated singular events” is helpful:

  • Today’s local weather may be unique, but data can be collected for thousands of dates and locations
  • Company performance is sensitive to specific circumstances but there are many companies and many time intervals over which performance can be measured
  • Similar policy interventions are attempted at different times in different jurisdictions etc.

Even if the event in question is truly singular, such as a pandemic, extreme solar storm or climate change, some “simpler” repeated events can be found or constructed.

You don’t need to commit to just one expert - several methods exist for combining forecasts:

  • Simple Averaging: each expert’s forecast is given equal weight. This method can be effective when there’s no basis for assigning different weights to different experts, or when all experts are equally competent.
  • Weighted Averaging: this approach assigns different weights to different experts based on their past performance, expertise, or other relevant factors.
  • Prediction Markets: In prediction markets, individuals bet on the outcomes of events. The market prices can then be interpreted as probability estimates for these events.
  • Extremising: This involves adjusting consensus forecasts towards the extremes and can be beneficial when the combined forecast is overly conservative due to the averaging process.
  • Structured Discussions: Experts engage in a structured discussion or debate to develop a combined forecast. This process can be facilitated by a neutral moderator.

Some thoughts

When approaching a problem in data science or mathematical modeling, consider these suggestions:

Identify the type of question you are dealing with:

  • Prediction would include propensities, demand forecasting, actuarial pricing etc.
  • Causal inference would include anything to do with estimating impact of a deliberate intervention.
  • Optimal control is trying to identify the “best” course of action.

Clearly identify the type of system you are modeling:

  • Are you dealing with frequentist or subjective probabilities?
  • Is there a reference class of events you can assess how well calibrated the subjective probabilities might be?
  • Is there a mechanism to aggregate subjective probabilities?

Decoupling of subjective probabilities and Bayesian updating and instead focusing on objective verification is a path to improving reliability of conclusions.

Even if we are uncertain about the probability of some very large risks and the efficacy of proposed interventions, we may still have solid ground for action if the downsides are sufficiently large, the so called precautionary principle.
